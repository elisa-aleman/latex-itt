\section{Introduction}\label{intro}

\DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{Japan has been known historically for its hospitality being the highest grade. The spirit of Japanese hospitality is celebrated around the world in a single Japanese word: }\textit{\DIFadd{Omotenashi}}\DIFadd{. With roots in Japanese history and tea ceremony, their hospitality is famous around the world \mbox{%DIFAUXCMD
\cite{al2015characteristics}}\hspace{0pt}%DIFAUXCMD
. Therefore it would stand to reason that tourists visiting Japan would have this hospitality as their first and foremost satisfaction factor. However, it is known that customers from different countries and cultures hold different expectations \mbox{%DIFAUXCMD
\cite{engel1990}}\hspace{0pt}%DIFAUXCMD
. Thus, it could be theorized that their satisfaction factors should be different. How will different cultures react and perceive hotels and their hospitality in this context? Our study attempts to bring light to this with two essential tourist populations that differ in culture to Japan: Chinese and Western tourists. 
}

\DIFaddend In the last couple of decades, the Japanese economy has been more and more affected by an increase in inbound international tourism \cite[][]{jones2009} with a Year-on-Year Growth Rate of 19.3\% in 2017, with a total of \num[group-separator={,}]{28691073} inbound tourists that year \cite[][]{jnto2003-2019}. From this total, the tourist population was mostly Asian (86.14\%), and approximately a fourth of the total (25.63\%) came from China. Western countries, counting English-speaking countries \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{in addition to }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{and }\DIFaddend the whole of Europe\DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{, }\DIFaddend make for 11.4\% of the total, with a 7.23\% of the total being countries where English is the official or the de facto national language. \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{Specifically for Chinese tourists , the effect }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{The effect of Chinese tourists }\DIFaddend on international economies \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{as well as }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{is increasing. From that, }\DIFaddend the number of researchers interested in this phenomenon has been increasing as well\DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{. }\DIFaddend \cite[][]{sun2017}. With these and other multicultural \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{tourists }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{tourist }\DIFaddend populations, the tourist market is more and more diverse. Diversity in customers' cultural backgrounds means that their expectations when staying at a hotel will also be varied. Hotel management\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{therefore }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{, therefore, }\DIFaddend needs to cater to these \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{different }\DIFdelend needs and expectations to increase \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{their customers' }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{customer }\DIFaddend satisfaction, maintain a good reputation, and \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{, therefore, }\DIFdelend generate positive word-of-mouth.

However, recent studies on social sciences, \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{which includes }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{and thus, on }\DIFaddend tourist behavior, have been performed using surveys on populations that could be culturally biased for the western world \cite[][]{nielsen2017, jones2010WEIRD, guaratne2009, hogan1978biases}. Those that do include Asian populations in their analysis, most commonly study Chinese tourist behavior \cite[e.g.][]{liu2019, chang2010, dongyang2015}\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{, and a }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{.  The }\DIFaddend few that compare Asian to western tourist behavior \cite[e.g.][]{choi2000} \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{, }\DIFdelend are commonly survey or interview-based studies with small samples, which\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{while valid }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{, while valid, }\DIFaddend can have its limitations. This \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{gap in research }\DIFaddend creates a need for cross-cultural studies for the increasing Asian and Western tourist populations. It could be said that Westerners make for a smaller portion of the tourist population compared to Asians\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{; however}\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{. However}\DIFaddend , according to \cite{choi2000}, Westerners are known as ``long-haul" customers, spending more than 45\% of their budget on hotel lodging\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{, compared to }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{. In comparison, }\DIFaddend their Asian counterparts only \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{spending }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{spend }\DIFaddend 25\% of their budget on hotels. \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{It is therefore important }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{Therefore, it is essential }\DIFaddend to study both Asian and Western tourist populations, their differences, and contrast the results with existing literature. 

With the advent of Web 2.0 and customer review websites, researchers realized the benefits of online reviews for research, and their importance for sales  \cite[][]{ye2009, basuroy2003}, customer consideration \cite[][]{vermeulen2009} and perception of services and products \cite[][]{browning2013}, among other effects of online interactions between customers \cite[e.g.][]{xiang2010, ren2019}. Consequentially, tourism research also began to use information collected online for data mining analysis, such as opinion mining \cite[e.g.][]{hu2017436}, predicting hotel demand from online traffic \cite[][]{yang2014}, recommender systems \cite[e.g.][]{loh2003}, and more. Data mining, machine learning, and big data methodologies can increase the number of manageable samples \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{per study. The increase can be }\DIFaddend from the hundred \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{or so }\DIFdelend samples manually analyzed by researchers to the hundreds of thousands that are automatically analyzed by machines. This \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{technology }\DIFaddend can not only help confirm existing theories but also lead to finding new patterns and to knowledge discovery \cite[][]{fayyad1996data}. 

In this study, we take advantage of the availability of enormous amounts of online reviews of Japanese hotels by both \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{Chinese }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{Mainland Chinese tourists posting in }\textit{\DIFadd{Ctrip}}\footnote{\label{ctrip}\DIFadd{Ctrip: }\href {\DIFadd{www.ctrip.com/}}{\path{www.ctrip.com/}}} \DIFaddend and Western English-speaking tourists populations \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{posting in }\textit{\DIFadd{TripAdvisor}}\footnote{\label{tripadvisor}\DIFadd{TripAdvisor: }\href {\DIFadd{www.tripadvisor.com/}}{\path{www.tripadvisor.com/}}}\DIFaddend . With this data, we can \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{both }\DIFdelend confirm existing theories about their differences in behavior, as well as perform an exploration of the data to discover factors that could have been overlooked in the past. \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{For thispurpose}\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{In order to do this}\DIFaddend , we use machine learning to automatically classify review sentences as positive or negative opinions of the hotel\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{, and }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{. We then }\DIFaddend perform a statistical extraction of the topics that \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{concerns }\DIFaddend the customers of each population \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{are mostconcerned about}\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{the most}\DIFaddend .

\section{Research objective}\label{research_objective}

\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{The objective of this study}\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{This study's objective }\DIFaddend is to determine the difference in \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{preferences between Chinese-speaking }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{factors driving satisfaction and dissatisfaction between Chinese }\DIFaddend and English-speaking \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{customers of }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{tourists in the context of high-grade hospitality of }\DIFaddend Japanese hotels using text-mining techniques. We aim to determine a quantitative measure of similarity \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{(and therefore, difference) }\DIFdelend between the satisfaction and dissatisfaction factors of both customer groups using a Rank-biased Overlap measure\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{. Furthermore, we will measure if both customer groups have differences in priorities for the factors that are common to both groups. In addition}\DIFdelend , \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{both in general and only considering their common factors. More importantly, }\DIFaddend we also \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{measure how }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{intend to measure how managerial and environmental attributes influence }\DIFaddend much satisfaction and dissatisfaction of both customer groups\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{are influenced by managerial attributes (internal }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{. We define managerial attributes as those internal and modifiable }\DIFaddend to the hotel\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{), as well as environmental attributes (external to the hotel )}\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{. In contrast, environmental attributes are external and unmodifiable, short of moving the hotel building in its entirety}\DIFaddend .

\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{Furthermore, our }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{Our }\DIFaddend proposal includes the use of large scale data from online hotel reviews in Chinese and English to study their differences in a statistical manner. In the past, survey-based studies have provided a theoretical background for a few specific tourist populations of a single culture or that travel with a single purpose\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{; this }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{. The short scope of those studies }\DIFaddend means that cultural and language differences often cannot be observed in a single study. 

\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{The difference in preferences that our study uncovers }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{Our study attempts to uncover the difference in satisfaction and dissatisfaction factors between cultures. These factors }\DIFaddend can become the focal point for making improvements in \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{the }\DIFaddend tourism and service industries \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{, increase the satisfactionof customers , and influence them to write more satisfied }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{and increase customer satisfaction. Satisfied customers will then write more positive }\DIFaddend online reviews that will\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{in turn }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{, in turn, }\DIFaddend increase sales and attract new customers. 

\section{Theoretical background and hypothesis development}\label{theory_hypothesis}

\DIFaddbegin \subsection{\DIFadd{Japanese hospitality: }\textit{\DIFadd{Omotenashi}}}\label{theory_omotenashi}

\DIFadd{The spirit of Japanese hospitality, or }\textit{\DIFadd{Omotenashi}}\DIFadd{, has roots in the countries history. However, to this day, it is regarded as the highest standard \mbox{%DIFAUXCMD
\cite{ikeda2013omotenashi, al2015characteristics}}\hspace{0pt}%DIFAUXCMD
. There is even a famous phrase in customer service in Japan: }\textit{\DIFadd{okyaku-sama wa kami-sama desu}}\DIFadd{, or translated ``The customer is god''. Some say that }\textit{\DIFadd{Omotenashi}} \DIFadd{originated from the old Japanese art of the tea ceremony in the 16th century. However, other scholars found that its roots come from even earlier, in the form of formal banquets in the 7th century \mbox{%DIFAUXCMD
\cite{aishima2015origin}}\hspace{0pt}%DIFAUXCMD
. The practice of high standards in hospitality has survived throughout the years. Today, it permeates all business practices in Japan, from the cheapest convenience stores to the most expensive ones. Manners, service, and respect towards the customer are taught to workers in their training, and high standards are always followed as to not fall behind in the competition. In Japanese businesses, hotels included, staff members are trained to speak in }\textit{\DIFadd{sonkeigo}}\DIFadd{, or ``respectful language", one of the most formal of the Japanese formality syntaxes. They are also trained to bow with different depths depending on the situation, where a light bow could be used to say: ``Please, allow me to guide you". Deep bows are also used to apologize for any inconvenience the customer could have, followed by a very respectful apology as well. In fact, despite the word }\textit{\DIFadd{omotenashi}} \DIFadd{being translated directly as ``hospitality'', it includes both the concepts of hospitality and service \mbox{%DIFAUXCMD
\cite{Kuboyama2020}}\hspace{0pt}%DIFAUXCMD
. 
}

\DIFadd{It stands to reason that this high level of hospitality would be a positive aspect that would be at the top of satisfaction for any customer. However, in Japan, every business has this high level of hospitality, in differing levels of success. A simple convenience shop around the corner could be more hospitable if hotel management falls behind. Businesses have to strive to be the most hospitable, and hotels are not allowed to lag in this competition. Because of this, other factors such as proximity to a convenience store, or transport availability, or perhaps room quality might be more critical to a customer. Customers can always achieve satisfaction from hospitality elsewhere, so hotels need to be competitive in their hospitality. 
}

\DIFadd{Therefore we pose two hypotheses for our study:
}

\begin{subhyp}
\begin{hyp}
\label{hyp:omotenashi_zh}
\DIFadd{Chinese tourists are most satisfied with Japanese }\textit{\DIFadd{Omotenashi}} \DIFadd{factors such as staff behavior or service.
}\end{hyp}

\begin{hyp}
\label{hyp:omotenashi_en}
\DIFadd{Western tourists are most satisfied with Japanese }\textit{\DIFadd{Omotenashi}} \DIFadd{factors such as staff behavior or service.
}\end{hyp}

\DIFadd{However, Japanese hospitality comes from Japanese culture. Different cultures interacting with it could have a different reaction. While some might be impressed by it, some might consider other factors more important to their stay in a hotel. This point leads us to a derivative of the above hypothesis:
}

\begin{hyp}
\label{hyp:omotenashi_both}
\DIFadd{Western and Chinese tourists have a different reaction to Japanese }\textit{\DIFadd{Omotenashi}} \DIFadd{factors such as staff behavior or service.
}\end{hyp}
\end{subhyp}

\DIFaddend \subsection{Customer satisfaction and dissatisfaction during hotel lodging}\label{theory_satisfaction}

Customer satisfaction in tourism has been analyzed since decades past, \cite{hunt1975} having defining customer satisfaction as the realization or overcoming of expectations towards the service. \cite{oliver1981} defined it as an emotional response to the provided services in retail and other contexts, and \cite{oh1996} reviewed the psychological processes of customer satisfaction for the hospitality industry. It is generally agreed upon that satisfaction and dissatisfaction stem from the individual expectations of the customer\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{, and as }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{. As }\DIFaddend such, \cite{engel1990} \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{state that satisfaction and dissatisfaction are therefore influenced by }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{states that }\DIFaddend each customer's background\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{. This is why Western and Asian, specifically Chinese , customers can }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{, therefore, influences satisfaction and dissatisfaction. Western and Chinese customers can then }\DIFaddend have very different factors of satisfaction and dissatisfaction since they have different backgrounds and cultures. These varying backgrounds will lead to varying expectations of the \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{services that a hotel can provide for them}\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{hotel services}\DIFaddend , the experiences they want to have while staying at a hotel, and the level of comfort that they will have\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{, }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{. These expectations will be there }\DIFaddend from the moment that they choose the hotel throughout their stay. These \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{contrasting }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{different }\DIFaddend expectations, in turn, will determine the \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{contrasting }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{distinct }\DIFaddend factors of satisfaction and dissatisfaction for each kind of customer, as well as the order in which they prioritize them. 
\DIFaddbegin 

\DIFaddend Therefore we propose\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{our first and second hypothesis}\DIFdelend :

\DIFaddbegin \begin{subhyp}
\DIFaddend \begin{hyp}
\DIFdelbegin %DIFDELCMD < \label{hyp:1}
%DIFDELCMD < %%%
\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \label{hyp:diff_all}
\DIFaddend Chinese and Western tourists have different priorities in hotel attributes that lead to their satisfaction and dissatisfaction.
\end{hyp}

\DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{Because of their different origins, expectations, and cultures, it stands to reason Chinese and Western tourists could have completely different factors to one another. Therefore, it could be that some factors do not appear in the other reviews at all. For example, between different cultures, it can be that a single word can express some concept that would take more words in the other language. So we must measure their differences or similarities at their common ground as well. For this, we prepare the following hypothesis:
}

\DIFaddend \begin{hyp}
\DIFdelbegin %DIFDELCMD < \label{hyp:2}
%DIFDELCMD < %%%
\DIFdel{The common satisfaction and dissatisfaction }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \label{hyp:diff_com}
\DIFadd{The common }\DIFaddend factors identified for both Chinese and Western tourists will be placed at a different priority for each group.
\end{hyp}
\DIFaddbegin \end{subhyp}
\DIFaddend 

Studies on customer satisfaction \cite[e.g.][]{truong2009, romao2014, wu2009} commonly use the Likert scale \cite[][]{likert1932technique} (e.g. 1 to 5 scale from strongly dissatisfied to strongly satisfied) to perform statistical analysis of which factors relate most to satisfaction on the same dimension as dissatisfaction \cite[e.g.][]{chan201518, choi2000}. \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{This }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{The use of the Likert scale }\DIFaddend leads to correlation analyses \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{(either multivariate or single variable ones) }\DIFdelend where one factor can lead to satisfaction, \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{while it is implied }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{implying }\DIFaddend that the lack of it can lead to dissatisfaction. However, a binary distinction (satisfied or dissatisfied) could allow us to analyze the factors that \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{solely }\DIFdelend correlate to satisfaction \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{, as well as exploring factors which }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{and explore factors that }\DIFaddend are solely linked to dissatisfaction. There are fewer examples of this approach, but studies have done this in the past \cite[e.g.][]{zhou2014}. \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{While it is true that this method can }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{This method can indeed }\DIFaddend decrease the extent to which we can analyze degrees of satisfaction or dissatisfaction\DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{. However}\DIFaddend , it has the benefit that it can be applied to a large sample of text data via automatic sentiment detection techniques using artificial intelligence. 

Previous research has also focused on factors that are controllable by the hotel managers and staff, i.e.\DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{, }\DIFaddend hotel services, staff behavior, or facilities \cite[e.g.][]{shanka2004, choi2001}\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{, without focusing }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{. However, little focus is put }\DIFaddend on factors that are uncontrollable by the hotel staff \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{, such as surroundings }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{that can play a part in the customers' choice behavior and satisfaction. Examples of these factors include the surroundings of the hotel}\DIFaddend , location, language immersion of the country as a whole, or \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{of }\DIFdelend touristic destinations, as well as \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{the }\DIFaddend integration of the hotel with tours available nearby, among other factors\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{that can play a part in the customers choice behavior and satisfaction. 
}\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{. 
}

\DIFaddend This leads to \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{our third }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{the next }\DIFaddend hypothesis:

\begin{hyp}
\DIFdelbegin %DIFDELCMD < \label{hyp:3}
%DIFDELCMD < %%%
\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \label{hyp:man_env}
\DIFaddend Satisfaction and dissatisfaction \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{stems }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{stem }\DIFaddend from both internal (managerial) and external (environmental) attributes of the hotel.
\end{hyp}

\DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{The resulting proportions of managerial attributes to environmental attributes for each population could serve as a measurement of how much the improvement of management in the hotel can increase future satisfaction in customers.
}

\DIFaddend \subsection{Chinese and Western tourist behavior}\label{theory_zh_en}

%DIF <  Asians vs Western behavior
%DIF >  Asians vs. Western behavior
In the past, tourist behavior analyzed from western samples \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{and surveys }\DIFdelend was wrongly thought to be a representation of universal behavior across all cultures \cite[][]{nielsen2017, jones2010WEIRD, guaratne2009, hogan1978biases}. Recently, however, with the rise of Chinese outbound tourism, both academic researchers and businesses have decided to study Chinese tourist behavior \cite[][]{sun2017}. This \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{results }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{increase resulted }\DIFaddend in several studies focusing on only the behavior of this subset of tourists. To this day, cross-cultural studies and analyses for Asian and Western tourists are scarce. \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{A few examples are }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{One example is }\DIFaddend \cite{choi2000}, where it was found that Western tourists visiting Hong Kong are satisfied more with room quality while Asians are satisfied with value for money\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{; }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{. Another is  }\DIFaddend \cite{bauer1993changing}, where Westerners prefer the hotel health facilities while the Asian tourists were more inclined to enjoy the Karaoke facilities of hotels, and both groups tend to have high expectations about the overall facilities\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{; as well as }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{. Another example is }\DIFaddend \cite{kim2000}, where American tourists were found to be individualistic and motivated by novelty, while Japanese tourists were found to be collectivist and motivated by prestige and family, with \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{an }\DIFaddend escape from routine and an increase in knowledge as a common motivator. 

One thing to note with the above cross-culture analyses is that they were performed before the year 2000. The current Chinese economy boom is making an increase in the influx of tourists\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{, but the question that could be posed is whether that boom }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{. However, that boom could have }\DIFaddend created a difference in the expectations of tourists\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{, and, as such, in their satisfactions and dissatisfactions }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{. In turn, that boom could have influenced their satisfaction factors }\DIFaddend when traveling. Another note is that these studies were performed with questionnaires in places where it would be easy to locate tourists, i.e.\DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{, }\DIFaddend airports. However, our study of online reviews takes the data that the hotel customers uploaded themselves.

\DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{More recent studies have surfaced as well. A cross-country study \mbox{%DIFAUXCMD
\cite{FRANCESCO201924} }\hspace{0pt}%DIFAUXCMD
using posts from U.S.A. citizens, Italians and Chinese tourists, determined using a text link analysis that customers from different countries indeed have a different perception and emphasis of a few predefined hotel attributes. According to their results, it appears that U.S.A. customers perceive cleanliness and quietness most positively and that Chinese customers perceive budget and restaurant above other attributes. Another couple of studies \mbox{%DIFAUXCMD
\cite{JIA2020104071, HUANG2017117} }\hspace{0pt}%DIFAUXCMD
analyze differences between Chinese and U.S. tourists using text mining techniques and more massive datasets, although in a restaurant context. 
}

\DIFadd{These last three articles focus on U.S.A. culture, while our study focuses on Western culture. Another difference with our study is that of the context of the study. The first study \mbox{%DIFAUXCMD
\cite{FRANCESCO201924} }\hspace{0pt}%DIFAUXCMD
has the context of tourists from 3 countries staying in hotels across the world, the second choosing restaurant reviews from the U.S.A. and Chinese tourists eating in 3 countries in Europe, and the third analyzing reviews of restaurants in Bejing. Our study, on the other hand, focuses on Western culture, instead of a single Western country, and Chinese culture clashing with the hospitality environment in Japan, specifically. The importance of Japan in this analysis comes from the unique environment of high-grade hospitality that the country presents. In this environment, do customers hold their satisfaction to this hospitality regardless of their culture, or are other factors are more important to the customers overall? Our study measures this at a large scale across different hotels in Japan. 
}

\DIFaddend % Universal behavior
Other \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{more recent }\DIFdelend studies, perhaps recognizing that samples being comprised of people from Western industrialized countries \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{aren't }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{are not }\DIFaddend representative, have gone further and studied people from many countries in their samples, and performed a more universal and holistic (not cross-culture) analysis. \cite{choi2001}, for example, analyzed hotel guest satisfaction determinants in Hong Kong with surveys in English, Chinese and Japanese translations, with people from many countries in their sample. \cite{choi2001} found that staff service quality, room quality, and value for money were the top satisfaction determinants. As another example, \cite{Uzama2012} produced a typology for foreigners coming to Japan for tourism, without making distinctions for their culture, but their motivation in traveling in Japan. In another study, \cite{zhou2014} analyzed hotel satisfaction using English and Mandarin online reviews from guests staying in Hangzhou, China coming from many different countries. The general satisfaction score was noticed to be different in those countries\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{, but a deeper }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{. However, a more in-depth }\DIFaddend cross-cultural analysis of the satisfaction factors wasn't performed. As a result of their research, \cite{zhou2014} thus found that customers are universally satisfied by welcome extras, dining environments, and special food services. 

% Western behavior 
Regarding Western tourist behavior, a few examples can tell us what to expect when analyzing our data. \cite{kozak2002} found that British and German tourists' satisfaction determinants while visiting Spain and Turkey were hygiene and cleanliness, hospitality, the availability of facilities and activities, and accommodation services. \cite{shanka2004} found that English-speaking tourists in Perth, Australia were most satisfied with staff friendliness, \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{the }\DIFaddend efficiency of check-in and check-out, restaurant and bar facilities\DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{, }\DIFaddend and lobby ambiance.

Studying Chinese tourists in Vietnam, \cite{truong2009} found that Chinese tourists are highly concerned with value for money. According to \cite{liu2019}, Chinese tourists tend to have harsher criticism when compared with other international tourists. \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{And }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{Moreover, }\DIFaddend as stated by \cite{gao2017chinese}, who analyzed different generations of Chinese tourists and their connection to nature while traveling, Chinese tourists prefer nature overall\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{, but }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{. Although the studies focusing only on Chinese tourists or only on Western tourists have a narrow view, their theoretical contributions are valuable. We can see that depending on the study and the design of questionnaires, as well as the destinations\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{, }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{; }\DIFaddend the results can vary greatly. \DIFaddend \subsection{Data mining, \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{machine learning, }\DIFaddend knowledge discovery and sentiment analysis}\label{theory_data}

%DIF <  Explain data mining and discovery of theory inside the data
%DIF >  Explain the discovery of theory inside the data
In the current world, data is presented to us in larger and larger quantities. Today's data sizes were commonly only seen in very specialized large laboratories with supercomputers a couple of decades ago\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{, but }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{. However, }\DIFaddend they are now \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{common }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{standard }\DIFaddend for market and managerial studies, independent university students\DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{, }\DIFaddend and any scientist that can connect to the Internet. Such quantities of data are available to study now more than ever\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{, but }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{. Nevertheless, }\DIFaddend it would be impossible for researchers to parse all of this data by themselves. As \cite{fayyad1996data} summarizes, data by itself is unusable until it goes through a process of selection, preprocessing, transformation, mining, and evaluation\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{, and only then it can }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{. Only then can it }\DIFaddend be established as knowledge. \subsection{SVM weight analysis}\label{svmweightsanalysis}

During the SVM learning algorithm, each \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{point of data }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{data point }\DIFaddend that is classified incorrectly causes a change in the weight vector to \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{better }\DIFdelend classify new data correctly. These changes to the weight vector are \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{strong for features that }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{greater for features close to the separating hyperplane. These features have stronger changes because they }\DIFaddend needed to be taken \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{account of }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{into account }\DIFaddend to classify with a minimal error\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{, those close to the separating hyperplane}\DIFdelend . Sequentially, the weight vector can be interpreted as a numerical representation of \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{the effect each featurehad for }\DIFdelend each \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{feature's effect on each }\DIFaddend class in the classification process. Below we show the formula for the weight vector \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{\(w\) }\DIFaddend (\ref{eq:svm_weight})\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{.
}\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{, where \(x\) is the training data and each vectorized sentence \(x_i\) in the data is labeled \(y_i\). Each cycle of the algorithm alters the value of \(w\) by \(\alpha\) to reduce the number of wrong classifications. This equation shows the last value of \(\alpha\) after the end of the cycle.
}\DIFaddend 

\begin{equation}\label{eq:svm_weight}
w = \sum_{i=1}^N \alpha_i y_i x_i
\end{equation}

\subsection{Rank-biased Overlap}\label{rbo}

In order to compare the similarity between two ranked lists, most cases would call to action a statistical measure such as Kendall's \(\tau\). However, \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{since }\DIFdelend the lists are not necessarily of the same length \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{, or have necessarily }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{or necessarily have }\DIFaddend the same contents\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{, and are }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{. They are also }\DIFaddend top-weighted (where the first rank is the most important to know, \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{with }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{and are }\DIFaddend less and less \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{importance }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{important }\DIFaddend as the ranks continue)\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{, }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{. Therefore, we propose that }\DIFaddend it is necessary to use another measure of similarity. \cite{webber2010similarity} proposed a Rank-biased Overlap (RBO) measurement\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{, which }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{. It }\DIFaddend takes all of these factors into consideration when calculating a value from 0 to 1, where \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{0 }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{zero }\DIFaddend means completely different lists\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{and 1 }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{, and one }\DIFaddend means the two lists are an exact match. Because our Chinese keywords (their translations, at least) \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{don't }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{do not }\DIFaddend match our English keywords one to one, it is necessary to use this method. Webber's RBO produces \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{3 }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{three }\DIFaddend measurements: a minimum RBO, a residual RBO\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{(from which one can }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{, and an extrapolated RBO.
The residual RBO allows us to }\DIFaddend know the maximum RBO\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{), and an extrapolated RBO (where the list is assumed to }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{. In contrast, the extrapolated RBO is calculated by assuming the list would }\DIFaddend continue in the same pattern of similarity towards infinity\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{)}\DIFdelend . The formula for the extrapolated RBO is shown in (\ref{eq:rbo_ext})\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{, Where }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{. }\DIFaddend \(S\) and \(T\) are listings, \(d\) is their depth, \(k\) is their evaluation depth, \(p\) is a parameter that controls the top-weightedness of the lists\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{(or it could be thought }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{, and \(X\) is the overlap between the lists. The evaluation depth \(p\) can also be understood }\DIFaddend as \(1-p\) being the probability to stop looking at the next item in the list\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{), and \(X\) being the overlap between the lists}\DIFdelend . The complete process is described at length by \cite{webber2010similarity}.

\begin{equation}\label{eq:rbo_ext}
RBO_{EXT}(S,T,p,k) = \frac{X_k}{k} \cdot p^k + \frac{1-p}{p} \sum_{d=1}^k{\frac{X_d}{d} \cdot p^d}
\end{equation}

. With bigger returns on managerial improvements, it seems like investing in improving attributes that dissatisfy Western customers, such as cleanliness and removing tobacco smell. Making more hotel facilities tobacco-free and deodorizing the rooms can be a low-cost investment that could increase returns many times over. 
}

\DIFadd{The opposite argument could also be made, however, that Chinese customers provide a more significant number of customers, even though they tend to spend less on lodging. Attracting a large number of Chinese customers can be a viable strategy for hotels. However, as mentioned before, they tend to focus more on environmental attributes, leaving language barrier-breaking as one of the few strategies to accomplish this.
}

\DIFadd{The basic premise of this study is that different cultures lead to different expectations and satisfaction factors. This premise also plays a role in the differentiation between the preference of managerial or environmental attributes. Perhaps the cultural background of Chinese tourists emphasizes their surroundings and their place in nature and the environment. Chinese historical backgrounds of Confucianism, Taoism and Buddhism permeate the thought processes of Chinese populations. However, scholars argue that the changes in generations and their economic and recent history gives less importance to these concepts in their lives \mbox{%DIFAUXCMD
\cite{gao2017chinese}}\hspace{0pt}%DIFAUXCMD
. However, one could argue that there is a Chinese cultural attribute that emphasizes the environment and the place one is in towards satisfaction, rather than the way one is treated. According to previous research, Chinese tourists are collectivist, while Westerners are individualists \mbox{%DIFAUXCMD
\cite{kim2000}}\hspace{0pt}%DIFAUXCMD
. A more anthropocentric and individualistic Western culture could result in more of their expectations and priorities be related to how one is treated in social circumstances, rather than the environment one is in. 
}

\subsection{\DIFadd{Implications for hotel managers}}\label{disc:implications}

\DIFadd{Our study presents two important conclusions: one about hospitality and cultural differences, and another about managerial decisions towards two different populations. Chinese tourists are not experiencing the best that }\textit{\DIFadd{Omotenashi}} \DIFadd{has to offer. Either they do not get much satisfaction from hospitality or feel that basic language and communication needs are not being met. On the other hand, Western tourists are elated with Japanese hospitality. The other conclusion is that managerial decisions will mostly benefit Western tourists, except that Chinese language improvements and breakfast inclusion can satisfy more Chinese customers. Japan is recently seeing an increase in Chinese students as well as Western students of universities. Hiring students as part-time workers could increase the language services of a hotel.
}

\DIFadd{To satisfy both customer types, hotel managers need to invest in cleanliness, deodorizing, and making hotel rooms tobacco-free. It could also be recommended to invest in breakfast inclusion and multilingual services and staff preparedness to deal with Chinese and English speakers. Western tourists were also observed to have high standards in comfort, which can be improved upon managerially for better reviews. Perhaps it could be suggested to perform surveys of the bedding that is most comfortable for Western tourists. However, not all hotels can invest in all of these factors simultaneously. Our results suggest that satisfying cleanliness needs can satisfy both customer types and that a low-cost investment could be to make the facilities tobacco-free. 
}

\DIFadd{Environmental factors, while not manageable after a hotel has finished its construction, are essential to consider for managers as well. As previously stated, transport systems and nearby shops are points of interest for both Chinese and Western tourists. Japanese hotel managers have to consider the location and surroundings since before the hotel is constructed. A suggestion could be to purchase land and start the construction after plans to make new subway lines are made. 
}

\DIFadd{It is left to the managers to consider their business model for the next strategy. One option could be attracting more Chinese customers in number with their observed low budgeting. Another could be attracting more high budget Western customers is on par with their business model. For example, investing more on cleanliness could improve on Western customers looking for high-quality lodging, even though the price per night would increase.
}


\DIFaddend \section{Limitations \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{and Future Work}\DIFaddend }\label{limitations}

This paper is not without its limitations. We analyzed keywords of satisfaction and dissatisfaction statistically based on whether they appeared on satisfied reviews or dissatisfied ones. Following that, we \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{performed observations of sentences with those words, in an attempt }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{attempted }\DIFaddend to understand the context that these words were being used in \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{by taking samples and observing them manually}\DIFaddend . However, because of the large number of sentences in our data being analyzed, we could not perform a complete analysis of the context of these sentences across the database. Another limitation is that a \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{big }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{large }\DIFaddend portion of the Asian tourists coming to Japan \DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{are }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{is }\DIFaddend Taiwanese and Korean\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{, of which we couldn't do an analysis because of team members not knowing }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{. }

\DIFadd{In the context of Japanese hospitality, we found that Western tourists had the most satisfaction with staff behavior, confirming our hypothesis \ref{hyp:omotenashi_en}. We also found that most of the Western customer satisfaction (\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{60}\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{50}\DIFaddend \% of the top 10 words) and dissatisfaction (100\% of the top 10 words) stems from managerial attributes of the hotel\DIFdelbegin \DIFdel{, while }\DIFdelend \DIFaddbegin \DIFadd{.
